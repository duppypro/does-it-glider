🛸 Glider Detection & Tracking Plan

Detecting a glider isn't just about matching a shape; it's about tracking a process through time. This plan outlines how we will move from a sparse grid to a collection of "identified" gliders.

1. The Detection Algorithm (Spatial)

Since we have live_cells, we don't scan the whole grid. Instead, we use each live cell as a potential constituent of a glider.

  • Step A: Iterate through live_cells.
  • Step B: For each cell, test it against the 4 phases (and 8 symmetries) defined in patterns.js.
  • Step C: If a match is found, tag those cells with a glider_id.

2. Identity Persistence (Temporal)

To avoid "counting" the same glider multiple times as it moves, we use Spatial Overlap Match.

Feature Mechanism
Unique ID New gliders get an incrementing_id.
Tracking In Gen N+1, look for a glider whose bounding box overlaps the bounding box of a glider from Gen N.
Displacement A glider moves at most 1 cell diagonally every 4 generations. We check a 3x3 search area around the previous center.

5. The "Solitary Glider" Problem

We are currently detecting patterns, but a glider is only a process if it is solitary. If other live cells are touching the glider, they act as "interference," causing the glider to crash or transform in the next generation.

6. Questions for Refinement

  • Strict Moat: Should we require a full 1-cell radius of "dead space" around the glider to count it?
  • Halo Size: A glider's "influence" is actually 3x3 for rules, but a 5x5 area covers its movement. Should the detection verify that the entire 5x5 bounding box contains only the glider cells?
  • The "Birth" Moment: If a glider is forming out of chaos but currently touched by a spark, do we wait until it is "free" to assign an ID, or do we count the spark as part of its history?

7. Sub-Agent Moat Analysis

To definitively answer whether a "strict moat" is necessary, a programmatic sub-agent was deployed to test all 1,048,576 possible configurations of the 20 adjacent moat cells in a 5x5 grid around a glider (testing the down-right orientation).

Glider Phase Tested Configurations Glider Survives + Debris Remains Glider Survives + Perfect Moat (No Debris)
Phase 0 1,048,576 128 2 (Empty moat, and 1 specific single-cell noise)
Phase 1 1,048,576 96 1 (Only the empty moat)
Phase 2 1,048,576 128 2 (Empty moat, and 1 specific single-cell noise)
Phase 3 1,048,576 96 1 (Only the empty moat)

Sub-Agent Findings:

  • Debris is inevitable: In ~100 configurations per phase, the expected 5 cells of the next glider phase survive, but the noise also survives or spawns new cells (debris). This means the glider is no longer solitary and will crash in subsequent generations.
  • The Single Exception: For Phase 0 and Phase 2, there is exactly one live cell configuration (a single live cell at coordinate 0,0—the top left corner) that dies instantly from underpopulation without affecting the glider's evolution.
  • Conclusion: Because 99.99% of adjacent live cell configurations destroy the glider, and the only "safe" noise is a single cell that vanishes instantly, enforcing a Strict Empty Moat (checking that all 20 non-glider cells in the 5x5 bounding box are dead) is the most accurate way to define a true, solitary glider.
